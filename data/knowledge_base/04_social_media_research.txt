Social Media Research: Impact on Teen Mental Health
Source: Journal of Adolescent Health, Twenge et al. (2018-2023), Haidt et al.

SECTION 1: KEY RESEARCH FINDINGS
Landmark studies on social media and adolescent mental health:

Twenge et al. (2018) - iGen Study:
- Analyzed data from 500,000+ US teenagers over 40 years
- Teen depression increased sharply after 2012 (smartphone adoption)
- Girls particularly vulnerable: 50% increase in depression rates 2012-2017
- Social media use correlated more strongly with unhappiness than almost any other activity

Haidt & Rausch (2022) - Social Media and Mental Health:
- Analyzed 17 systematic reviews and meta-analyses
- Strong correlational evidence for harm, particularly in girls
- Mechanisms: social comparison, cyberbullying, sleep disruption, displacement of in-person interaction

Braghieri et al. (2022) - Facebook and Mental Health:
- Quasi-experimental study using staggered Facebook rollout on US campuses
- Facebook introduction increased depression and anxiety diagnoses by 7-9%
- First causal evidence linking social media to mental health decline

SECTION 2: MECHANISMS OF HARM

Upward Social Comparison:
- Social media creates a "highlight reel" environment
- Adolescents compare their behind-the-scenes to others' best moments
- Leads to feelings of inadequacy, low self-worth, and FOMO
- Girls experience stronger effects due to appearance-focused content

Displacement Effect:
- Each hour on social media displaces in-person social time
- In-person interaction is strongly protective for mental health
- Adolescents who spend 5+ hours on devices have 2x more depression symptoms

Attention Economy Effects:
- Platforms optimize for engagement through variable reward schedules
- Creates compulsive checking behavior similar to gambling addiction
- Average teen checks phone 150+ times per day

SECTION 3: PROTECTIVE USE OF SOCIAL MEDIA
Not all social media use is harmful. Research identifies protective factors:
- Active participation (creating, messaging) vs passive consumption (scrolling)
- Close-friend communication vs unknown influencer following
- Time-limited purposeful use vs open-ended browsing
- Content that builds skills or community vs content promoting comparison

SECTION 4: RISK FACTORS BY USAGE PATTERN
Research-based risk classification:
- LOW RISK: Under 1 hour/day, primarily messaging close friends, no nighttime use
- MEDIUM RISK: 1-3 hours/day, mixed active/passive use, occasional nighttime use
- HIGH RISK: 3+ hours/day, predominantly passive consumption, regular bedtime use, cyberbullying exposure
