Exercise, Physical Activity, and Adolescent Mental Health
Source: American Psychological Association, NIMH Research Summaries

SECTION 1: EXERCISE AS MENTAL HEALTH INTERVENTION
Physical activity is one of the most evidence-backed interventions for adolescent mental health. Research consistently shows:

- 30 minutes of moderate exercise 3x per week reduces depression symptoms by 47%
- Regular exercise reduces anxiety symptoms by up to 48% (meta-analysis of 49 studies)
- Exercise improves self-esteem and body image, key protective factors for teen mental health
- Physical activity increases BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), promoting neural growth

SECTION 2: TYPES OF BENEFICIAL EXERCISE
Aerobic Exercise (Cardio):
- Running, cycling, swimming, dancing
- Increases serotonin and dopamine naturally
- Recommended: 150-300 minutes per week of moderate activity
- Most effective for reducing depression and anxiety

Resistance Training:
- Weight lifting, bodyweight exercises
- Improves self-efficacy and stress tolerance
- Reduces cortisol (stress hormone) levels
- Recommended: 2-3 sessions per week

Team Sports:
- Basketball, football, volleyball, etc.
- Additional benefit: social connection reduces isolation
- Competitive element builds resilience and goal-setting skills

SECTION 3: EXERCISE AND BRAIN CHEMISTRY
How exercise affects mental health biochemistry:
- Endorphins: Natural pain killers, create "runner's high" euphoria
- Serotonin: Mood stabilization, regulated sleep, appetite control
- Dopamine: Motivation, reward, pleasure - naturally elevated without addiction risk
- Norepinephrine: Stress response regulation, improves stress resilience
- BDNF: Neuroplasticity factor, literally grows new brain connections

SECTION 4: SEDENTARY BEHAVIOR RISKS
Physical inactivity independent risk factor for mental health:
- Each hour of sedentary time above 8 hours increases depression risk by 8%
- Sedentary adolescents have 3.4x higher odds of anxiety compared to active peers
- Breaking sedentary time with short activity breaks improves mood and cognitive function

Exercise guidelines and risk classification:
- LOW RISK: 150+ minutes moderate exercise per week, less than 6 hours sitting daily
- MEDIUM RISK: 60-150 minutes per week, 6-8 hours sitting daily
- HIGH RISK: Less than 60 minutes per week, more than 8 hours sedentary daily
