Sleep Science and Adolescent Mental Health
Source: National Sleep Foundation, American Academy of Sleep Medicine

SECTION 1: RECOMMENDED SLEEP DURATION
Adolescents aged 13-18 require 8-10 hours of sleep per night for optimal physical and mental health. Current research shows that:
- Only 20% of teenagers get adequate sleep on school nights
- Chronic sleep deprivation is linked to a 3x increased risk of depression
- Social media use in the bedroom is the leading cause of teen sleep disruption

SECTION 2: SLEEP AND MENTAL HEALTH CONNECTION
Sleep and mental health have a bidirectional relationship:

Depression and Sleep:
- Insomnia is present in 75% of depressive episodes
- Poor sleep quality increases negative emotional reactivity by 60%
- Adequate sleep improves antidepressant treatment efficacy

Anxiety and Sleep:
- Sleep deprivation activates the amygdala (fear response center) by up to 60%
- REM sleep is essential for emotional processing and memory consolidation
- Chronic sleep debt creates a state of hyperarousal similar to anxiety disorders

SECTION 3: SLEEP QUALITY METRICS
How to assess sleep quality:
- Sleep latency: Time to fall asleep (healthy: under 20 minutes)
- Sleep efficiency: Time asleep / Time in bed x 100 (healthy: over 85%)
- Sleep consistency: Same bedtime and wake time daily
- Sleep continuity: Less than 2 awakenings per night

SECTION 4: SLEEP IMPROVEMENT STRATEGIES
Evidence-based interventions for better adolescent sleep:
1. Consistent sleep schedule - same time on weekdays and weekends
2. No screens 1 hour before bed - blue light suppresses melatonin by up to 50%
3. Cool, dark bedroom environment (18-20°C optimal)
4. No caffeine after 2pm - caffeine half-life is 5-6 hours
5. Physical exercise in the morning or afternoon (not within 3 hours of sleep)
6. Relaxation techniques: progressive muscle relaxation, deep breathing

Risk indicators related to sleep:
- Less than 6 hours/night: HIGH RISK for mental health deterioration
- 6-7 hours/night: MEDIUM RISK, borderline insufficient
- 8-10 hours/night: LOW RISK, within recommended range
- More than 10 hours regularly: May indicate depression (hypersomnia)
